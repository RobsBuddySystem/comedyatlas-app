Module 2: Writing Your First Jokes: A Practical Method, Not a Muse
2026-07-21 · COMEDY ATLAS

You do not need to wait for inspiration. Every craft-focused comedy-writing book agrees on that much, and several of them hand you a near-mechanical process to get a first draft of a joke on paper. This module walks through the two most widely taught frameworks, plus where working comics say the real material actually comes from.

Start from what's already true

Judy Carter's *The Comedy Bible* opens from the idea that your best material is not invented, it's excavated — she has students list what she calls their "comic premise": the specific, true, embarrassing, or absurd details of their own life, then reframes them through what she calls "the game," the exaggerated logic a bit runs on once the true premise is set up. The exercise she's known for is blunt: write down the worst things about yourself and the people around you, honestly, before worrying about whether any of it is funny yet. Funny comes from specificity, not from trying to sound like a comedian.

Melvin Helitzer's *Comedy Writing Secrets* gives a more mechanical checklist for the same problem, built around an acronym he called THREES: Target, Hostility, Realism, Exaggeration, Emotion, Surprise. His argument is that a joke is nearly always aimed at some target, carries a little real hostility toward it, is grounded in something realistic enough for an audience to recognize, pushed into exaggeration, wrapped in emotional stakes, and lands via a surprise the setup didn't telegraph. If a joke you've written feels flat, Helitzer's method is a diagnostic: which of the six is missing?

The mechanics of the punchline itself

Greg Dean's *Step by Step to Stand-Up Comedy* is the most technical of the twelve books this guide draws from, and its core idea is worth learning even if you never read the whole book: every joke has a connector — a specific word or short phrase in the setup that the audience interprets one way, and which the punchline suddenly reveals also means something else. The setup's job isn't to be funny; it's to build one clear, ordinary interpretation so the punchline's reinterpretation lands as a genuine surprise. If your punchline isn't landing, Dean's method says: look at your connector. Is there really a second meaning hiding in it, or are you just tacking a random funny idea onto an unrelated setup?

Don't write in a vacuum — write toward a room

John Vorhaus's *The Comic Toolbox* frames every joke as the gap between "comic reality" and "real reality" — the skewed, exaggerated logic a bit runs on once you've set up what's actually true. His practical advice is to write fast and generate volume before judging anything: get twenty bad ideas on paper to find the two good ones, rather than trying to write one perfect joke from a blank page.

Mike Sacks's *Poking a Dead Frog*, a collection of interviews with working comedy writers (from *SNL* and *The Simpsons* to stand-up itself), repeats one piece of advice across almost every interview subject: the material that survives is the material you actually test out loud, not the material that reads well silently on a laptop. A joke is a hypothesis about what a room will find funny. You don't know if you're right until you say it to a room — which is exactly what module 3 helps you find, and where module 5 gives you the format for your first set.

A first-session exercise

Combine the above into something you can do tonight: pick one true, specific, slightly embarrassing thing about your week (Carter's premise). Run it through THREES — who's the target, where's the real emotional stake, what's the exaggeration (Helitzer). Then find the connector: is there a word in your setup that quietly means something else once your punchline lands (Dean)? Write five versions. Most will be bad. That's expected — Vorhaus and Sacks both say the volume is the point, not a failure mode.
