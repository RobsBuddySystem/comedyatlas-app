Module 8: Building Your Five, Then Your Ten
2026-07-21 · COMEDY ATLAS

At some point your open-mic reps stop being about individual jokes and start being about a set: five fixed minutes, then ten, built from the material that's actually survived being said out loud to a room, in the order that makes those minutes work as a unit rather than a list.

Choosing what survives

Not every joke that got a laugh once belongs in your five. The bar for a fixed set is consistency: a bit that's landed across several different rooms, on several different nights, with several different crowds. A bit that killed once in a room full of exactly the right people and died everywhere else since is a room-specific bit, not a five-minute-set bit, at least not yet. This is the practical output of the review loop from module 6 and the bombing diagnosis from module 7 — you're using your own tape and your own notes to sort "worked once" from "reliably works."

Ordering for momentum

The widely shared circuit convention — reflected in comics' own submission-tape advice as much as in teaching books — is to open with something that establishes who you are quickly and reliably gets a laugh within the first thirty seconds, and to close with your strongest bit, not your newest one. A five-minute set built like a story, where each bit connects thematically to the next rather than reading as a random list of unrelated premises, is what bookers and audiences alike respond to best; that's echoed directly in booker-facing advice on what makes a submission tape land (module 10 covers this from the booking side). The middle is where you can afford a slightly riskier or newer bit, bracketed by material you already trust.

Writing transitions, not just standing there

A set isn't five separate jokes performed back to back — the gap between bits is part of the performance too. Melvin Helitzer's THREES framework (Target, Hostility, Realism, Exaggeration, Emotion, Surprise) is usually taught joke-by-joke, but it applies at the transition level too: a transition line that shares a target or an emotional thread with the bit before it feels like a continuation, not a hard reset. Even a single connective sentence — "speaking of things I'm bad at" — does more work than silence and a pause while you mentally flip to the next topic.

Doubling a five into a ten

The temptation, expanding a working five into a ten, is to just add more material at the same bar you used originally. The stronger approach, consistent with Stephen Rosenfield's analysis of how working comics actually build longer sets, is to look for the bits inside your five that have room to grow — a premise that got a laugh but had more angles you didn't explore, a tag you cut for time that actually worked when you tried it once — and expand those, rather than bolting five new unrelated minutes onto the end. A ten that's really "two fives stapled together" reads that way to a room; a ten that grew out of its own five feels like one continuous set.

Knowing when a bit is closed

Greg Dean's connector-based method gives you a concrete test here: once a bit's setup and punchline are tight — the connector's hidden meaning lands as a genuine surprise every time, not just sometimes — further rewriting risks making it worse, not better. The signal that a bit is "closed" for now isn't that you've gotten bored of it; it's that it's been consistently landing across enough different rooms that further tinkering is chasing marginal gains instead of fixing an actual problem.
