Module 7: Handling Bombing
2026-07-21 · COMEDY ATLAS

Bombing — a set met with silence, or close to it — happens to every comic who has ever done this seriously, including the ones cited throughout this guide. It is not a signal that you should quit, and treating it as one is the most common reason talented first-timers never come back for a second set. This module is about what to actually do with it.

It's data, not a verdict

Ali Wong has described working open mics nearly every night at San Francisco's Brainwash Cafe before *Baby Cobra* made her famous, testing new jokes and, by her own account, bombing regularly — because bombing in a room that doesn't matter yet is the only reliable way to find out which of your jokes are actually funny before it counts. The reframe every working comic eventually adopts is the same one Vorhaus and Dean apply to joke-writing itself: a joke is a hypothesis, and a room's silence is simply the result of the test. It tells you the hypothesis was wrong, or wrong for that room, or wrong in that phrasing. It does not tell you anything about whether you should be doing this.

*I Killed: True Stories of the Road from America's Top Comics*, the oral history compiled by Ritch Shydner and Mark Schiff from dozens of working comedians, includes a widely told story about George Wallace taking a young, visibly shaken comic aside after a bad set and telling him, in effect, that a bombed set is a fork: it either pushes you out of the business, or it makes you work hard enough that it stops happening as often. The point of including it in a book full of comics who went on to long careers is not subtle — the ones who lasted are the ones who treated the bad set as the second option.

Finish the set anyway

The instinct after a joke dies is to speed up, cut material, or get off stage early. Working advice across the circuit, and the discipline Rosenfield's *Mastering Stand-Up* teaches around timing and structure, points the other way: finish your time. A bombed opening bit doesn't predict a bombed closer, and cutting your set short gives you less tape (module 6) to actually learn from and signals to the room that you've given up on yourself before they have.

Diagnose the joke, not yourself

The useful question after a bad set is never "am I funny." It's narrower: was this joke's premise unclear, was the connector (Dean's term for a joke's hidden double meaning) not actually there, was the timing off, or was this a room that just wasn't going to respond to this angle regardless of execution — Jay Sankey's *Zen and the Art of Stand-Up Comedy* treats exactly this kind of self-observation, separating the performance from the self performing it, as a skill in its own right, not something that comes naturally. Sometimes the honest answer is the joke needs a rewrite. Sometimes it's that this particular room, this particular night, wasn't the right test — a work trip crowd, a late slot after twenty other comics, a room that skewed older than your material assumes. Both are useful to know. Neither is a verdict on you.

The only proven fix

Every comic's account this guide draws from agrees on the same unglamorous next step: get back on a list the next week. Not to prove something to the room — nobody in that room will remember your set by then — but because the fix for one bad set is more stage time, not less. This is the same stage-time math from module 1, applied to the moment it's hardest to believe in.
