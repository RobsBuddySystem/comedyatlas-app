Fifteen Years In, Still "Foreign": Sebastian Marx's Bilingual Stand-Up
2026-07-18 · COMEDY ATLAS · Comic Spotlight

Fifteen years in France. A French partner, three kids, a PACS on file at the mairie. And Sebastian Marx still speaks French, by his own admission, "with a German tourist accent." That one detail tells you where his comedy actually lives — not in the easy zone of "look how weird France is," but in the stranger territory of being fully embedded in a country that still hears you as foreign the moment you open your mouth.

Marx didn't set out to build a bilingual career. He was doing low-stakes stand-up in New York and Boston when he followed a French girl back to her country — "I followed a French girl who I met in New York," as he tells it. That was the whole plan. Somewhere in the years since, French stopped being a hobby and became a stage language: he started performing in it about a decade in, once he had the vocabulary to build real jokes, and now works both English and French sets.

What makes the act interesting isn't "American confused by baguettes." It's a guy who no longer gets to use foreignness as an excuse. As his own show materials put it, after fifteen years he's "done for: he's right here" — dealing with "all the problems of a forty-something Franco-Jewish-New Yorker PACSED with 3 small children," a daughter who corrects his French, a home life that's completely, unglamorously French, and an accent that never fully clears customs. He's disarmingly honest about the mechanics of it too: "I still make mistakes and still feel limited in vocabulary and general wit" — a rare admission from a comic, and part of why the English sets in particular feel unguarded rather than polished-to-death.

He's also had to relearn how to read a room. French audiences, in his account, are "more reserved," slower to jump in, and favor humor that's "a bit more refined, with plays on words and social commentary" over anything broadly vulgar. Even within France he's clocked a split: crowds outside Paris run "more expressive and excited," while Parisians are "more judgmental and harder to please." That's a decade of recalibrating in real time, in two languages, in front of two very different audiences.

That code-switching instinct is exactly why he fits a room built for this collision. Marx plays French Fried Comedy Night at Velvet Bar, our flagship English/French show pairing American and French comics on the same bill — a stage set up for precisely the bilingual tightrope he's been walking his whole career.
