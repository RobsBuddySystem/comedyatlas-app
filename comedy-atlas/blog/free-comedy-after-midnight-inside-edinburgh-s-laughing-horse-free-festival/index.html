Free Comedy After Midnight: Inside Edinburgh's Laughing Horse Free Festival
2026-07-19 · COMEDY ATLAS

Edinburgh in August turns into one enormous comedy room, but the Laughing Horse Free Festival is the version of it that doesn't ask you to pick a show in advance, budget for a ticket, or plan your evening around a single venue. It's a scattered, pay-what-you-can network of shows tucked into the backs and basements of ordinary Edinburgh bars — the kind of pubs that were serving pints long before the Fringe existed and will keep serving them long after. You walk in off the street, find a room you didn't know was there, and sit down for comedy.

That's the whole appeal, and it's a real one. The Laughing Horse rooms aren't polished 500-seat theatres with a marketing budget behind them — they're back rooms and basements like The Three Sisters' Wee Room and Maggie's Front Room, the Nineties room at The City Cafe, the Main Cellar under West Nic Records, and Flight Club tucked into the West Port Oracle. Some nights it's the Attic or the Lounge at The Counting House; other nights it's the Basement Bar at Boston Bar or the Cinema Room at Brass Monkey. Each has its own footprint and its own slightly cramped, slightly sticky-floored charm — the kind of room where a comic can see every face in the crowd and where a heckle actually lands.

What sets this festival apart from the ticketed Fringe machine is the format: these are free-to-enter, pay-what-you-want shows, and the lineup leans hard into short, sharp, late-night sets rather than one big-name hour. On a single Thursday night in August, you could catch six different shows within a 35-minute window — that's not a hypothetical, it's the actual schedule. Three Bad Sisters at 1:25am in The City Cafe's Nineties room. Sex! with Becky Fury five minutes later in the Wee Room. Toxic Femininity, Struan Logan's Chaotic Good, and more, all stacked back to back to back across different corners of the same few blocks. It rewards wandering. If one room's material isn't your speed, another show is starting two doors down before you've even finished your pint.

The range on offer is genuinely wide, which is part of the fun of a free festival — there's no single voice curating for a mainstream Fringe crowd, so the bill runs from broad, silly premises to pointed, personal material. There's Alingon Mitra's Clean Cuts and Elliot Wengler's Jokemon for something more character-driven and referential. There's Almost 50 AND a Woman and Grandad & Me Do Stand Up Comedy for a generational spin on the format — proof this isn't strictly a young comic's game. There's Dr Jimmy: First Steps Into the Gay-niverse and Wiki Knows Best – The Rest Is Islands for comics working a specific, personal angle rather than generic crowd-work. And then there's the stuff built purely to be a late-night release valve — 100% F***ed Up and Far From Home Comedy Showcase, Comedy Striptease, the whole loose, slightly chaotic after-midnight energy that defines this end of the festival's schedule.

Prices, where they exist at all, sit around the £5 mark, and several shows are genuinely free entry with a bucket collection at the end — so a night out here can mean five or six shows for the price of a couple of pints. That makes it a natural fit for anyone doing the Fringe on a real budget, or anyone who's already spent the day's money on a big-ticket show and wants to keep the night going without further damage to their wallet. It's also a good fit for comedy-curious visitors who don't have a specific comic in mind and just want to feel what live Edinburgh comedy is actually like at street level — unpolished, immediate, and packed into rooms that were never built for it but work anyway.

The late start times — most of these shows run past 1am — mark this out as the after-hours leg of the Fringe circuit, not the warm-up. This is where the night goes once the bigger venues have cleared out, when the crowd is looser, the comics are looser, and a five-minute walk between two different basements counts as a full evening's entertainment. If you're the type who'd rather stumble into a good surprise than book a seat months out, this corner of Edinburgh in August is built for exactly that.

Full listings and showtimes for individual nights, including Three Bad Sisters, Sex! with Becky Fury, 100% F***ed Up and Far From Home Comedy Showcase, Comedy Striptease, Toxic Femininity, and Struan Logan: Chaotic Good, can be checked closer to the date — rooms and slots in a free festival like this tend to shift night to night.
